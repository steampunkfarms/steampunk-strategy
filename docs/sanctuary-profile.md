## Zones & Facilities

### Main Pot Belly Yard (~2.5 acres — ~150 pot belly pigs)

The barn is a converted stable originally built for eight horses, split into several areas for the pigs. This is the largest single zone on the property.

**Shelter inventory:**
- Three 15×15 ft stable stalls
- Several stainless steel porta-huts oriented south for winter sun, protected from the strong Ranchita winds (west/east) and especially the very strong storm winds from the northwest
- ~12 "pig-loos" (repurposed dogloos) for less sociable pigs — often abuse-case rescues that prefer solitude
- 10 wood-construction pig houses (4×8 ft; some split, some open interior) where small daily or friend groups settle in each night

**Shade infrastructure:** Large pinwheel arrangement of shade sails strung between a hub-and-spoke system of steel poles (volunteer-installed). Different colored shade sails keep it fun looking and introduce color.

**Notable residents in this zone:**
- **Fanny** — A farm hog who lives with the pot bellies. She is gentle with the smaller pigs, offers body heat to supplement cold-season straw bedding, and serves as an extra line of protection against coyotes. She has three pot-belly/farm-hog mix "lieutenants" who are also large, gentle, and protective.
- **Muffin's crew** — Muffin and 5 others have their own area under the trees. Muffin gets ornery but loves his crew. He has a permanent joint injury and a bad attitude, so separation from the main herd works better.

**Sub-areas within the main yard:**
- Two segregated areas for pot bellies that become less food-competitive and need their own house and separation from the main herd

### Tuscany (Low-Mobility Area)

Two stable stalls on the east end, arranged for lower-mobility pigs (both pot belly and senior farm hogs). Less food competitiveness in this zone. A small labyrinth of cubbies has been constructed inside the stalls so every resident can have as much or as little privacy as they want.

### Farm Hog Stalls (~1 acre)

Three stable stalls dedicated to farm hogs. They have about an acre of space with trees, big wallows, and several shade shelters.

### Main Goat Pasture (~3 acres)

Home to the majority of goats, the donkeys, Jazzy the retired thoroughbred racehorse, and two of the cows. Multiple steel structures provide shade and storm protection. A nearly 72-foot-long shelter houses hay troughs so animals can eat during rainy weather.

**CL Herd** — About a half acre with their own shelter, segregated to prevent infection of the other goats.

### Special Needs / Observation / Service Yards (~1 acre, on the main house parcel)

**ICU** — Inside the house by the heater for the most critical animals.

**Special Needs Yard** — For goats needing daily wheelchair use, pigs that are weak/sick/very elderly and need daily care.

**Observation Yard** — Transitional yard; special needs animals pass through here before rejoining a main herd. Two permanent residents:
- **Sylvester** — Huge Duroc with hip issues. Lacks mobility but makes up for it as the biggest brother in the observation yard — lets animals snuggle him on colder nights.
- **Chance** — Quick friend to all; the observation yard is a perfect fit for his personality.

**Service Yard** — For low-mobility but otherwise healthy pigs and goats that never seem to get along in the larger pastures. Two protectors:
- **Peppa Jr** — Huge and snuggly
- **Pumpernickel** — Big and snuggly too

### Boar Pens (4 zones — intact males separated from females)

Segregated by size and temperament.

| Zone | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **The Eight** | Smallest boars — runting issues or very elderly where neuter is too risky. Named because there were originally eight. |
| **The 88** | Adjoins The Eight. Less mobile and weaker boars needing shade/shelter. More than 8 but not 88 — the name just stuck. |
| **Main Boar Yard** | Mix of all sizes, awaiting the next round of neuter funding (last funded in 2024). Numbers have grown slowly as intact males arrive from LA, Orange County, and San Bernardino kill shelters not suited to house farmed animals. |
| **Angus (solo)** | One intact male who lives alone — he is a jerk to humans, other pigs, and seems to generally hate being anything but alone. ~500 sq ft of space with his own shelter. His only companion is a lone jungle fowl that doesn't like anyone else either. |

### Chicken Coops (4)

Housing rescued battery hens, chickens saved from slaughter, and roosters from LA shelters where raids on rooster-fighting rings landed them in the shelter system. The sanctuary still takes these roosters when they come along — Krystal is exceptional at taming roosters that spent their lives being trained as attack roosters for gambling rings.

### Jungle Fowl

Origin unknown — suspected stealth dumping or strays from a neighboring property. They roam freely, eat bugs, and roost in the tall cedar tree by the house. Super pretty but keep mostly to themselves.

**Known dumping history at the sanctuary:** 10 pigs left in crates in the driveway one snowy night, Jute with his foot bound in twine, at least 6 goats thrown over the east fenceline, two cats left by the fence down from the main driveway.

### Quarantine Zone

Currently houses the Mulefoot 7 — intake from a Riverside shelter.

### Hay Shed

Converted 40×30 ft shop (originally for servicing fleet vehicles) with a 22-foot-high roof. Stores 500–700 bales of hay plus extra supplies.

### Expansion Parcel (10 acres, acquired Fall 2025)

Four acres currently being prepped for residents from the main goat pasture — more space, ample forage, and rotational pasturing to keep forage fresh as seasons drive rotation.

**Planned developments:**
- Olive trees on part of the acreage — high-value product to help fund animal care
- Pomace oil from olives → bio-diesel as clean-burning fuel for farm vehicles and generators
- Shelters from the current main goat pasture will move with the animals (except the long 72-ft hay trough shelter which stays)
